304 — House Rules

Jump to the one-page cheat sheet → · See house-rule differences from standard 304 →

“304” (pronounced “three-nought-four”) is a four-player partnership trick-taking card game with bidding, popular in Sri Lanka. The total point value of all cards in the pack is 304, hence the name. The game belongs to the Jass family, likely brought to Sri Lanka by Dutch traders in the 17th or 18th century. 304 is the probable ancestor of the Indian games 28 and 29.

This ruleset describes the house rules as played within our group, with certain variations from the standard rules in effect. Where our rules diverge from the standard game, these are marked as House Rule. Rules have been adapted from the Pagat website and by Aings Aingaran.

Objective

The goal is to win the match. Each team begins with 10 stone (non-playable point indicator cards, 2–6 of all suits, separated by colour). The first team to give away all their stone wins the match. Stone is given or received at the end of each game based on whether the betting team met their bid.

Units of Play

A round is a single trick (all four players play one card each). A game consists of 8 rounds, at the end of which a team wins or loses stone. A match is a series of games, ending when one team has given away all 10 stone. A revolution is where all combinations of players play against each other across multiple matches to determine the overall winner.

A match typically lasts 30 minutes to an hour. A revolution (3 matches covering all player combinations) runs 3–4 hours.

Phases of a Game

Each game proceeds through the following phases:

  1. Shuffling and First Deal — The dealer shuffles and deals the first batch of 4 cards to each player.
  2. 4-Card Betting — Players bid on their first 4 cards.
  3. Choosing Trump — The highest 4-card bidder places one of their cards face down to indicate trump.
  4. Second Deal — The dealer deals the remaining 4 cards to each player.
  5. 8-Card Betting — A second round of bidding after all 8 cards are dealt.
  6. Play — 8 rounds of trick-taking.
  7. Scrutiny — Teams count points and determine whether the betting team met their threshold. Caps calls are verified. Stone is exchanged accordingly. This phase can involve spirited deliberation.

Players & Cards

There are 4 players in teams of 2. Partners sit opposite each other. Deal and play proceed anticlockwise.

304 uses a 32-card pack consisting of 7, 8, 9, 10, J, Q, K, A in all four suits. The rank of cards from high to low in all suits, and their point values, are:

Note that J has precedence over 9, which has precedence over A, and so on. 8 beats 7, even though both are worth 0 points. The total across all suits is 304 (76 per suit × 4).

Setup

The deck is sorted into the pack (32 playable cards, 7–A) and the stone (the remaining 20 cards, 2–6). Each team receives 10 stone, sorted by colour. The pack is shuffled. Any player may deal first — the choice is informal (random or by group convention).

Stone Holder

Each team designates one stone holder at the start of the match. The stone holder is the official custodian of their team’s stone pile and the authoritative party for stone exchange during scrutiny. By convention, the two stone holders sit adjacent to each other so stone passes directly between neighbouring seats — the typical arrangement is the player to the dealer’s right and their cross-table opposite (i.e. one player on each team, seated next to each other in the rotation).

The role persists for the duration of the match unless the team agrees to swap mid-match. Stone holders are responsible for: keeping the team’s pile sorted by colour and visible, executing stone exchange at end of game, and arbitrating count disputes from their team’s side during scrutiny.

Dealing

The dealer shuffles the pack, performing 1–4 overhand shuffles. Minimal shuffling is preferred — this preserves the order of cards from the previous game to encourage high-value betting. Once shuffled, the player to the left of the dealer may cut the pack once, or tap the top of the deck to decline the cut. No further shuffles from the dealer after the cut.

The dealer then deals the top 4 cards to the player on their right, then 4 to the next player (anticlockwise), and so on, finishing with themselves. This deals half the pack (4 cards each).

Between games, the dealer rotates one seat anticlockwise.

Riffle remedy. If three consecutive 4-card redeals occur at the same seat (i.e. the priority player or their partnered partner triggers a redeal three deals in a row), the dealer may perform one riffle shuffle to break up clumped low cards before the next deal. The trigger threshold is at the players’ discretion — the table may agree to riffle earlier or later.

Betting & Choosing Trump

Betting on 4 Cards

Betting begins with the player to the dealer’s right. The minimum opening bid is 160 (commonly said as “60” — by convention, the leading “1” is dropped for bids below 200). Bids must increase in increments of 10 (below 200) or 5 (200 and above).

On a player’s turn, they may take one of three actions. Each counts as a speech (a turn in the bidding):

  • Bet — Declare a bid higher than the current highest bid (with sufficient increment). This signals the player believes their team can win at least that many points.
  • Pass — Decline to bet.
  • Partner — Ask your partner to bid in your place. The partner immediately takes a turn in your seat (bet or pass), and that bid/pass is treated as their first speech. House Rule Partnering is restricted to:
    • Phase: 4-card betting only — never on 8 cards.
    • Player: only the player to dealer’s right or the player across from dealer. Their respective partners (the dealer-left player and the dealer themselves) cannot partner.
    • Speech: only on that player’s first speech.
    • No partner-back: the partnered player cannot return the partner action.

Skips

Bidding proceeds anticlockwise. When bidding reaches a player, they are skipped (silent — no bid, no pass, no event) under either of the following conditions:

  • §B-1 Partnered-player skip. Their first speech was used by their partner asking them to bid. Their normal turn is skipped exactly once (the next time bidding reaches their seat). On subsequent circuits, they speak normally.
  • §B-2 Current-bid-holder skip. They currently hold the highest bid. They are skipped on every subsequent turn until another player overtakes the highest bid, at which point they re-enter rotation.
  • §B-3 A skipped turn is silent: it neither advances nor resets the consecutive-pass counter.

Termination

§B-4 Bidding ends when both of the following are true:

  • Every player has had a first speech (a bid, pass, or partner action — including a partnered bid/pass, which counts as the partner’s first speech).
  • Three consecutive passes have occurred since the last bid (or since the start of bidding if no bid has been made).

§B-5 Any bid (including a partnered bid) resets the consecutive-pass counter to zero. Skips do not affect the counter. Once 3 consecutive passes have accumulated and all 4 players have had a first speech, bidding ends — even if a skip would otherwise have been actuated next. The skip is not a “free turn” the bid-holder can use to extend the auction; once the termination conditions are met, the auction is closed and the bid-holder wins at their last bid.

Edge case. A holds the highest bid. B passes, C passes, D passes (counter = 3, all four spoke). Bidding ends. A is not given a “next turn” via skip — the auction is over, A wins at their last bid. The current-bid-holder skip exists to bypass A while bidding circulates, not to grant A an extra speech once the auction has closed.

If termination occurs without any bid having been placed, a pass-on is triggered (see below).

Constraints on Bidding Below 200

Bids below 200 are subject to two constraints:

  • §B-6 First speech only. A player can bid below 200 only on their first opportunity to speak. After they have bet, passed, or had their first speech consumed via partnering, their minimum bid becomes 200.
  • §B-7 Cannot undercut your own partner. If your partner currently holds the highest bid, you cannot bid below 200 — you must bid 200 or higher to take over, or pass. Bidding below 200 to overtake the opposing team’s bid (e.g. bidding 180 over their 170 on your first speech) is fine.

There is no upper limit on 4-card bids in principle — a player could theoretically bid 220 or higher, including PCC, on 4 cards. In practice, this is extremely unwise (you are committing without seeing 4 of your cards), and bids of 220+ on 4 cards are virtually never made.

Worked Example — 4-Card Betting (Simple) ▼

Players are seated anticlockwise from the dealer: A (right of dealer), B, C (A’s partner), D (B’s partner, dealer).

  1. A bids 60 (160). A’s first speech. Counter: 0.
  2. B bids 70 (170). B’s first speech, increment of 10. Counter: 0 (reset by bid).
  3. C passes. C’s first speech. Counter: 1.
  4. D passes. D’s first speech. Counter: 2. All 4 have now spoken.
  5. A’s second turn. A has spoken, minimum is 200. A bids 100 (200). Counter: 0.
  6. B bids 105 (205), increment of 5 above 200. Counter: 0.
  7. C passes (1). D passes (2). A bids 110 (210). Counter: 0.
  8. B passes (1). C passes (2). D passes (3). Counter hits 3 → bidding ends. A wins at 210.

In practice, the trailing “C passes, D passes” in step 8 are formalities and rarely spoken aloud once a bid is established and the next speaker has already passed. Formally they are still required for the counter to reach 3.

Worked Example — 4-Card Betting (Partnering) ▼

Same seating. A has cards in multiple suits and no strong trump preference.

  1. A says “partner”. C (A’s partner) immediately bids in A’s seat. C bids 60 (160). Both A’s and C’s first speeches are now used.
  2. B bids 70 (170). B’s first speech. Counter: 0.
  3. C’s normal turn — skipped (partnered-player skip; used once). Counter unchanged.
  4. D passes. D’s first speech. Counter: 1. All 4 have now spoken.
  5. A’s second turn. A has spoken; minimum 200. A passes. Counter: 2.
  6. B’s turn — skipped (B holds the highest bid at 170). Counter unchanged.
  7. C’s turn (back in rotation; partnered-skip already used). C has spoken (via partnering); minimum 200. C bids 200. Counter: 0.
  8. D passes (1). A passes (2). B passes (3). Counter hits 3 → bidding ends. C wins at 200.

Alternative scenario with no bid: A=partner → C=pass, B=pass, (C skip), D=pass. Counter advances to 3 at D’s pass; all 4 have spoken; no bid was placed → pass-on.

Redeal on 4 Cards (low points)

A redeal (the same dealer reshuffles and deals again) may be triggered if:

  • The player to the dealer’s right (priority player), or House Rule their partner if the priority player partnered to them, holds 4 cards totalling less than 15 points (i.e. 14 or lower); and
  • They declare it during the 4-card betting phase, before the second deal.

Standard rules restrict this to the priority player only; our group extends to the partnered partner. Redeal is optional — a brave player may continue.

Pass-on on 4 Cards (no bid placed)

If 4-card betting terminates with no bid placed, a pass-on occurs: the deal moves to the next dealer (anticlockwise).

Choosing a Trump Suit

The highest 4-card bidder selects one of their 4 cards and places it face down on the table without showing it. This is the Trump card. The suit of this card is the Trump suit for the game. The highest bidder is now the Trumper. The Trumper must not look at their remaining 4 cards before selecting the Trump card.

If 8-card bidding later supersedes the 4-card bid, the new highest bidder picks up any prior Trump card and selects a fresh one from their full 8-card hand (placed face down).

Not to be confused with the 45th & 47th President of the United States of America, Donald J. Trump.

Betting on 8 Cards

After the Trump card is placed, the dealer deals the second batch of 4 cards to each player (same dealing order). After everyone has seen their 8 cards, a second round of betting begins.

House Rule 8-card betting begins with the player to the dealer’s right, the same as 4-card betting. (Standard rules start with the highest bidder from the 4-card round.)

House Rule On 8 cards, players may only bet or pass — partnering is not allowed. (Standard rules permit partnering on 8 cards; we do not.)

The minimum 8-card bid is 220, referred to as “Honest” House Rule (standard rules set the 8-card minimum at 250). If the existing 4-card bid is 220 or higher, the 8-card bid must exceed it. Bids from 220 onwards proceed in increments of 5. Bids above Honest are named accordingly: Honest 5 (225), Honest 10 (230), and so on up to Honest 25 (245). A bid of 250 is simply called “250.”

The skip and termination mechanics from 4-card betting apply identically to 8-card betting (current-bid-holder skip; bidding ends after all 4 first speeches and 3 consecutive passes).

If no player bids on 8 cards — which is the most common scenario — the 4-card bid remains the prevailing bet and play proceeds accordingly.

If any player bids on 8 cards, this supersedes the 4-card bid entirely. The 4-card Trumper picks up their Trump card. The new highest 8-card bidder becomes the Trumper and selects a new Trump card from their 8-card hand. The original 4-card Trumper may also overbet on 8 cards if they wish to remain Trumper (or to change their Trump suit).

Constraints on Bidding Below 250

The same first-speech constraint as 4-card betting applies, with 250 as the threshold:

  • §B-8 First speech only. A player can bid below 250 only on their first opportunity to speak in the 8-card round. After speaking, their minimum becomes 250.
  • §B-9 Cannot undercut your own partner. If your partner holds the highest 8-card bid, you must bid 250 or higher to take over.

The only legal bid higher than 250 is PCC (Partner Closed Caps).

Pass-on on 8 Cards (low points)

House Rule A player may elect a pass-on if their hand totals less than 25 points on 8 cards. This must be declared on the player’s first 8-card speech — once they have bet, passed, or been skipped past, the option is waived. Pass-on moves the deal to the next dealer.

Absolute Hand

House Rule An absolute hand is one so strong that the holder is guaranteed to win all 8 rounds before play begins, regardless of how others play and regardless of priority order. This is exceptionally rare (well under 1% of hands).

Two paths exist:

  • Goodwill declaration. The holder lays their hand face up before round 1 and announces an absolute hand. A pass-on is triggered (next dealer), no stone is exchanged, and the holder forfeits the (otherwise certain) Caps reward. Most players play goodwill.
  • Post-game claim by opposition. If the opposition lost every round and can demonstrate that no permutation of their play would have won them any round, they may claim absolute hand during scrutiny. If accepted by group consensus, the result is reversed to a pass-on with no stone exchanged. No penalty for an incorrect claim — the burden is on the opposition to make a convincing case.

Partner Closed Caps (PCC)

PCC is the highest possible bid. When a player bids PCC:

  • Their partner puts down their hand and is no longer in play for all 8 rounds.
  • The Trumper plays Open Trump (see below). Unlike other open-trump games, the PCC Trumper is not required to lead with Trump on round 1 — they may lead any card. Trump is revealed via the standard “pick up folded card, reveal any trump-suit card to others, return to hand” mechanism before play begins.
  • The Trumper has priority for the first round. Play proceeds anticlockwise as normal, skipping the Trumper’s partner.
  • Caps does not need to be called — the bid itself is staked entirely on winning all 8 rounds. Caps and External Caps mechanics do not apply to PCC.
  • The Exhausted Trumps rule does not apply.
  • PCC + Absolute Hand interaction. If the PCC Trumper holds an absolute hand, they may declare it (goodwill) before round 1. This triggers a pass-on with no stone exchanged — the same outcome as a non-PCC absolute hand declaration. The PCC stake is not paid out. Post-game opposition claims of absolute hand are not available in PCC (the partner is out of play and so cannot claim, and the trumping side either won and is paid the PCC stake, or lost and pays it).

House Rule PCC rewards/punishes 5 stone (win 5 / lose 5). Standard rules award 4 for a win and 5 for a loss.

Play

The player to the dealer’s right has priority for the first round — they lead by playing a card face up. Thereafter, the winner of each round gains priority and leads the next. The other three players each play a card in turn (anticlockwise). (PCC: the Trumper has priority for round 1; the Trumper’s partner is out for the entire game.)

Following suit is mandatory. Players must play a card of the same suit that was led (the led suit) if they have one. If they cannot follow suit, they may play any card.

The round is won by the highest Trump card played to it, or if no Trump was played, by the highest card of the led suit. Points from cards in each won round accumulate towards the team’s total for the game.

Between rounds, players may inspect the face-up cards from the most recently completed round only — not any earlier rounds, and not during the middle of a round in progress. In Closed Trump, any cards that were played face down and not revealed by the Trumper remain face down and may not be inspected.

Open Trump Games

After 8-card betting and before the first round, the Trumper may elect to play Open Trump. The declaration mechanic depends on whether the Trumper has priority for round 1:

  • Trumper has priority for round 1. The Trumper picks up the folded Trump card and returns it to their hand, then must lead round 1 with a card of the Trump suit (any trump-suit card from hand, not necessarily the originally folded one). Leading the trump card serves as the reveal.
  • Trumper does not have priority for round 1. The Trumper picks up the folded Trump card, returns it to their hand, then reveals any card of the Trump suit to the other players (not necessarily the originally folded one). The revealed card returns to hand and is not visible thereafter — players who fail to remember the trump suit later have only themselves to blame; requests for re-confirmation will be denied.

From the moment of declaration, all cards are played face up and basic play rules apply.

House Rule PCC exception: the PCC Trumper plays Open Trump but is not bound to lead with Trump on round 1. They may lead any card.

House Rule In standard rules, bids of 250 or higher require the Trump card to be revealed at the end of the first round, with play proceeding as Open Trump from the second round onwards. We do not play this rule — Closed Trump games remain closed regardless of the bid amount.

Closed Trump Games

Most games are played as Closed Trump. The Trump card remains face down from the betting phase. While the Trump card is face down, any player who cannot follow the led suit must play their card face down (folded).

Playing a card face down is either an attempt to cut (play a card of the Trump suit to win the round) or a minus (play a worthless non-Trump card, conceding the round). Since other players do not initially know the Trump suit, cutting involves guesswork and skill.

Constraints on the Trumper in Closed Trump

  • §T-1 No trump-lead on round 1 from priority. The Trumper cannot lead with the Trump suit on the first round when they have priority. If the Trumper wishes to open with Trump, they must declare Open Trump before play begins.
  • §T-2 When the folded Trump card may be played. The Trump card itself can only be played:
    • Face down, to cut a non-Trump round led by another player, or
    • In the 8th round, when it is the Trumper’s last remaining card.
  • §T-3 Cut-or-minus when unable to follow. If the Trumper cannot follow the led suit, they may choose to:
    • Cut using the folded Trump card (played face down), or
    • Minus — fold a non-led, non-Trump-suit card. Useful when the Trumper’s partner is winning the round, or when the Trumper wants to discard a constraining card.
  • §T-4 No folding in-hand trumps. The Trumper cannot fold an in-hand trump-suit card. In-hand trump-suit cards (those not on the table as the folded Trump card) may only be played face up — never folded. Concretely, the Trumper’s face-down options are restricted to (a) cutting with the folded Trump card or (b) minusing a non-trump-suit card. If the Trumper holds only trump-suit cards in hand plus the folded Trump card, and is unable to follow a non-trump led suit, they must cut with the folded Trump card (no minus is possible).
  • §T-5 Trump-led, in-hand trumps available. If the led suit happens to be the Trump suit and the Trumper holds in-hand trump-suit cards (in addition to the folded Trump card on the table), the Trumper must follow suit normally with a face-up in-hand trump card. The standard “follow suit if able” rule applies — the folded Trump card on the table does not count as being “in hand” for the purpose of following suit, but the in-hand trumps do, and they must be played face up like any other follow-suit card. (This may incidentally reveal that the led suit is the trump suit, but the obligation to follow suit overrides any informational concern.)
  • §T-6 Trump-led, only the folded trump remains. If the led suit is the Trump suit and the Trumper has no Trump in hand except the folded Trump card on the table, the Trumper cannot follow suit (the folded Trump card cannot be played face up while it remains the indicator on the table). They must minus a non-trump-suit card face down.

Resolving Folded Cards

At the end of each round, if any cards were played face down, the Trumper inspects them privately:

  • No Trump among the folded cards: The folded cards remain face down and no one else may look at them. The round is won by the highest card of the led suit.
  • At least one Trump among the folded cards: The Trumper reveals all Trump cards that were played face down to that round, regardless of which one wins. The folded Trump card on the table is also flipped face up. The round is won by the highest Trump played to it. If the folded Trump card itself was not played to the round, it is shown to all players, then picked up and added to the Trumper’s hand. Any non-Trump folded cards (including a Trumper’s minus) are not revealed.
  • After the Trump card has been revealed, the Trump is said to be open. From this point forward, all cards are played face up and basic play rules apply.

Missing the cut: If a player attempts to cut but plays the wrong suit (not the Trump suit), their card has no power. A sign of bad things to come.

Overcut: If multiple players attempt to cut and more than one plays a card of the Trump suit, the highest Trump card among them wins the round. The winner is said to have overcut the others. All trump-suit cards played to the round are revealed; non-trump folded cards remain hidden.

Exhausted Trumps

If the Trumper has priority, leads with a Trump card, and no other player can respond with the Trump suit (meaning the Trumper holds all remaining Trump), the Trumper must continue leading all remaining Trump from their hand before leading any other suit.

This rule applies in both Open and Closed Trump games. In Closed Trump it can only meaningfully fire after Trump has been revealed (the Trumper cannot lead the folded Trump card; only in-hand trumps can be led). In practice it applies in rounds 3–7 of closed trump — round 2 leading-out implies Spoilt Trumps (no opposition trumps from the deal), and round 8 is the trivial final-card case.

The rule does not apply in PCC.

Spoilt Trumps

If the Trumping team’s opponents collectively hold zero Trump cards from the deal (i.e. between their 16 cards, not a single card belongs to the Trump suit), any player who notices may declare Spoilt Trumps. The game is void — no stone is exchanged, the cards are thrown in, and a pass-on triggers (next dealer).

Either team may call Spoilt Trumps at any time before the last card of the last round is played. After that, it is too late.

The safeguard principle. Spoilt Trumps, Absolute Hand, and the low-points pass-ons all sit in the same family: they are safeguards available to the disadvantaged side to void a deal that has gone irrecoverably against them. The general theme is that the side being disadvantaged carries the onus of invocation:
  • If the opposition is being crushed by a Spoilt-Trumps deal (no Trump = no defensive cuts), they will almost always call it.
  • If the Trumping team is somehow losing a Spoilt-Trumps deal (e.g. the Trumper’s partner holds catastrophic non-trump cards and the opposition is winning on raw point values), the Trumper may deduce the spoilt state from their partner’s trump plays and call it themselves to void the loss.
  • Either side is permitted to call. The losing side has the strongest motive.

Clarification: If the opposition originally held Trump cards but lost them through cuts during play (i.e. Trump was “pulled from them”), this does not count as Spoilt Trumps. The opposition must have held zero Trump from the outset of the game.

False Spoilt Trumps call: Calling Spoilt Trumps is itself a “speech” — it can divulge that the caller has been counting trumps. A false call (the opposition did in fact hold trumps) attracts a 1-stone penalty (caller’s team receives 1 stone). In practice this is exceedingly rare.

Caps

Winning all 8 rounds is called Caps. Winning all 304 points but losing a zero-point round does not count as Caps — it is purely about winning every round.

When to Call Caps

A player must call Caps at the point where, given all information available to them (cards played, cards in hand, suits opponents are exhausted of, etc.), there exists a specific order in which they can play their remaining cards to guarantee winning all remaining rounds, irrespective of how any other player chooses to play.

The critical distinction is between choice and deducible certainty. You cannot rely on your partner making an optimal or intelligent play — you cannot assume they will choose to play a particular card when they have options. However, if you can deduce from the cards played that your partner holds a specific card, and the rules force a guaranteed outcome (e.g. your partner holds the last remaining card of a suit and must therefore win a round when that suit is led), then you can factor that into a Caps call. The test is whether the outcome is guaranteed by the rules alone, not by hoping your partner plays well.

This is what makes Caps genuinely difficult — and why it has not been well implemented in online versions of the game. It requires not just tracking your own hand, but deducing the state of all hands from the history of play.

When calling Caps, the player puts their cards down and states the order of play. The opposition may scrutinise to verify there are no holes in the claim.

In strict play, Caps should be called on the precise card at which certainty is first achieved — potentially mid-round. In practice, calling Caps before the calling player makes their next play is acceptable, giving a brief grace period to recognise the situation.

Caps cannot be called after the final card of round 8 is played. Once the game ends, the call window is closed. Scrutiny may retroactively determine that a Caps situation was missed and apply Late Caps (see below), but no new call can be made.

Worked Example — Caps ▼

Trumps are Clubs. It is the start of Round 5 (4 rounds played). Your hand:

J♣9♣A♣J♥

From the previous rounds, you know:

  • All non-Club Jacks and 9s have been played.
  • Both opponents are out of Clubs (they failed to follow suit in Round 3).
  • One Heart (the 9♥) remains unaccounted for, held by an opponent or your partner.

Can you call Caps? You have priority. You lead J♣ — no one can beat it (you hold the top 3 Clubs and opponents are out of Clubs). Then 9♣, then A♣. On Round 8, you lead J♥ — the highest remaining Heart. You win all 4 remaining rounds regardless of what anyone plays.

You must call Caps now, before playing your card in Round 5. The order is: J♣, 9♣, A♣, J♥.

If you had waited until Round 6 to call Caps, that would be Late Caps. If you had called Caps in Round 4 (before seeing what opponents played in Round 4), that could be Early/Wrong Caps — you didn’t yet have enough information to guarantee the result.

Worked Example — Caps via Partner Deduction ▼

Trumps are Spades. It is the start of Round 7 (6 rounds played). You won Round 6, so you have priority. Your hand:

9♦7♦

From the previous rounds, you know:

  • All Spades (Trump) have been played out — no cutting is possible.
  • Both opponents are out of Diamonds.
  • You have tracked every Diamond. The only Diamonds remaining are the J♦ (which your partner must hold, since all other players are out of Diamonds) and your 9♦ and 7♦.
  • By elimination, your partner’s other card is the A♥ — the last remaining Heart.

Can you call Caps? You lead 9♦. Partner must follow suit with J♦ (their only Diamond). J♦ is the highest remaining Diamond — partner wins Round 7 and gains priority. Partner then leads Round 8 with A♥ (their only remaining card), the highest remaining Heart. Partner wins Round 8.

You must call Caps now. Even though your partner wins both remaining rounds, you are not relying on their choice. Every outcome is forced: partner must follow Diamonds with J♦, opponents cannot beat it (out of Diamonds, no Trumps), and partner’s last card is the highest remaining Heart.

Note: If you merely suspected your partner held the J♦ but could not deduce it with certainty, this would not be grounds for Caps.

Correct Caps

  • §C-1 Calling Caps correctly, at the first opportunity, and before the start of the 7th round, rewards the team with +1 stone on top of the normal win (e.g. a correct Caps on Honest = 2 + 1 = 3 stone given).
  • §C-2 After the start of the 7th round, a player must still call Caps at the first opportunity, but a correct call attracts no bonus — the win is scored normally. (By the 7th round, there is typically enough information for any competent player to recognise Caps, so no reward is given.)

Late Caps

  • §C-3 Definition. Late Caps occurs in either of two cases:
    • (a) The player calls Caps after the first opportunity at which they could have called correctly; or
    • (b) The team wins all 8 rounds during play without ever calling Caps, and scrutiny determines a first opportunity existed during play.
  • §C-4 Trumper standard. The Trumper is held to a higher standard than the partner. The Trumper has full visibility of every played card (including folded cards revealed at end of round); by the final rounds the Trumper should know what they hold and what opponents have left, so a missed Caps from the Trumper’s seat is rarely defensible.
  • §C-5 Penalty. Late Caps is punished by loss + 1 stone on top of the usual loss for the bid, even if the team won enough points to meet their original bid (e.g. Late Caps on Honest = 3 + 1 = 4 stone received by the betting team). A Late Caps loss flips the game outcome: even though Caps was achieved physically, the team scores it as a loss. House Rule Standard rules treat late and early Caps identically as “Wrong Caps.”

Wrong/Early Caps

  • §C-6 Definition. Wrong/Early Caps occurs when:
    • The player calls Caps too early (a prescribed order of play exists, but the opposition still has a way to win a round); or
    • The player calls Caps incorrectly (e.g. misremembering which cards have been played, or forgetting that the opposition has already won a round); or
    • The player deliberately throws a round to conceal a Caps situation (revealed during scrutiny by analysis of card play).
  • §C-7 Penalty. Punished by 5 stone. House Rule Standard rules distinguish between Wrong Caps where you still win all tricks (2 token penalty) and losing a trick after calling Caps (5 token penalty). We collapse both into 5 stone.
  • §C-8 Caps loss supersedes points. A Caps loss supersedes the points threshold for regular betting — if you failed to call Caps correctly but won more than enough points for your bid, this is still a loss punishable by the Caps penalty.

External Caps (Caps from Opposition)

  • §C-9 The non-Trumping team may also call Caps when they win all 8 rounds. The same “deducible certainty” test applies.
  • §C-10 PCC carve-out. External Caps does not apply to PCC (PCC is decided purely on the Trumper winning all 8 rounds).
  • §C-11 Window. External Caps must be called during play, no later than before the final card of round 8 is played.
  • §C-12 Lenient standard. The external team’s information is more limited than the Trumper’s (they have not seen folded cards, and have less ability to deduce hand contents), so scrutiny applies a more lenient “first opportunity” standard than for the Trumping team.

External Caps Outcomes

  • §C-13 Correct External Caps before the start of Round 7. The external team’s win is bumped — the betting team gives +1 additional stone on top of the normal loss (e.g. on a 160 bid, betting team gives 2 + 1 = 3 stone to the external team).
  • §C-14 Correct External Caps in or after Round 7. No bonus — normal opposition win is scored, betting team gives the usual stone for the bid amount.
  • §C-15 Late External Caps. The external team won all 8 rounds but, in scrutiny’s judgement, had a first opportunity to call Caps during play and missed it. Even though they won every round physically, they lose the game on the technicality, and the betting team takes a win + 1 stone outcome (e.g. on Honest, betting team gives 2 + 1 = 3 stone to the external team). This is harsh by design, exceedingly rare in practice, and exists to ensure the external team takes External Caps seriously when they have the deductive ability to recognise it.
  • §C-16 Wrong External Caps. The external team called Caps but the call doesn’t hold up under scrutiny (the prescribed order can be broken, or the external team had not in fact won every round). The external team takes a 5-stone penalty (receives 5 stone).

Caps Formalism — What “Deducible Certainty” Actually Means

An advanced-player article. Last updated 2026-05-01.

The Caps section above tells you when to call. This article tells you why. It is a precise account of the test scrutiny applies, written for intermediate and expert players who want to argue Caps from first principles. Beginners can ignore it: the headline rule (“there exists an order of play that wins every remaining round, given everything I can deduce”) does the work for normal play. The formalism only matters when a borderline situation goes to scrutiny, or when you want to step through possible worlds at the table to convince yourself a call holds.

Read the article — Caps Formalism ▼

Why Caps is hard, in one paragraph

Caps is not a fact about the deal. It is a fact about your information set — what you, sitting in your seat, can deduce from your own hand plus the public record of play. Two players at the same table can become Caps-obligated at different moments, because they know different things. The Trumper, who has seen every folded card revealed at end of round, often knows more than their partner; the external team usually knows least. A naive test — “given the actual deal, is there a winning order?” — is the wrong test. That asks whether an omniscient observer sees a sweep, not whether you can deduce one. In bridge terminology that is double-dummy analysis, and it is too generous: it gives Caps to a player who, given what they actually know, could still lose. The correct test is single-dummy: does there exist a play order that wins against every deal consistent with what you know, and against every legal continuation by the other seats? Single-dummy is strictly stricter, and it is the only test that matches the rules’ phrase “given all information available to them, irrespective of how any other player chooses to play.”

What you know

Your information set at any moment is the union of:

  1. Your own hand.
  2. Your own play history — including face-down plays you made, since you know what you put down.
  3. The public face-up history — every face-up card played by anyone, in completed rounds and the in-progress round.
  4. Public face-down revelations — folded trumps revealed at end-of-round resolution become public the moment they are flipped.
  5. Public suit-exhaustion — every player who failed to follow a led suit is publicly known to be out of that suit from then on, because the closed-trump rule mandates folding only when one cannot follow.
  6. The Trumper’s privileged observations — if you are the Trumper, you saw the identity of every face-down card at end of round, including opposition cuts, opposition minuses, and your partner’s minuses. Your partner and the opposition did not.

Note the asymmetry. The Trumper’s information set is larger than anyone else’s, which is exactly why the rules hold the Trumper to a stricter “first opportunity” standard (§C-4). The external team’s information set excludes line 6, which is why scrutiny is more lenient on them (§C-12). The same predicate, evaluated against a smaller information set, automatically becomes more lenient — there is no special case.

The space of consistent worlds

A world is a hypothesis about every card whose location is currently hidden from you. It assigns an identity to every card in every other seat’s hand and to every face-down completed-round card whose value you don’t already know. A world is consistent with your information if:

  • Cards conserve. Every card in the 32-card pack appears in exactly one place.
  • Hand sizes are right. Each remaining seat has the right number of cards for the round number.
  • Suit-exhaustion holds. No seat known to be out of suit s is given a card of suit s.
  • Hidden minuses obey the rules. A folded card whose identity you don’t know cannot be a card the player could have followed with — neither the led suit (they would have followed) nor, in most cases, trump (a trump fold would have been revealed at round end).
  • Known cards stay put. Cards whose identity you have already seen appear where you saw them.

The set of consistent worlds is finite. Early in a game it is large; mid-to-late game, suit-exhaustion typically prunes it heavily. The actual deal is always one of these worlds — but only one.

The Caps obligation, formally

You are Caps-obligated at the current state if and only if:

  1. Precondition. Your team has won every round so far.
  2. Witness. There exists a fixed order O of your remaining cards such that…
  3. …for every world consistent with your information, and…
  4. …for every legal continuation by the other seats (including the worst conceivable play),
  5. the playout from now — with you playing O in turn and the others playing freely — ends with your team winning every remaining round.

Three subtleties matter at the table:

  • The order is fixed up front. When you call Caps, you expose your hand and announce an order. You do not get to react to opponents’ choices. So the obligation is the existence of a committed order that survives all worlds and all adversaries.
  • No “reasonable” assumption. Opponents may play their absolute worst card every turn. The rules say “irrespective of how any other player chooses to play,” and the formal test takes that literally.
  • Forced plays count. If a world or the rules force a unique legal play (your partner has only one card of the led suit, say), the universal quantifier over opponent strategies collapses to that single play. This is how “deducible certainty via partner” works — not a special case, just a consequence of quantifying over legal moves.

First opportunity and timing

Your information only changes on observation events — when a card is played, when round resolution flips a folded trump, when (as Trumper) you inspect folded cards at end of round. Between events, your information set is static, so your Caps obligation cannot flip from false to true.

The first obligation state is the earliest such event at which the obligation predicate becomes true. The Late Caps judgement is then a function of when you actually called, relative to that first obligation state, under one of three timing policies:

  • Strict. The call must be placed at the first obligation state, before any further observation event.
  • Lenient (rules default, §C-3). The call may be placed at any moment up to and including your next own play. You may not let one of your own plays go by without calling.
  • Unified-time. A configurable wall-clock window after the first obligation state — used in fast play, not in scrutiny.

Scrutiny applies the lenient policy by default. The strict policy is the analytical ideal but rarely enforced in practice.

One predicate, four mechanics

The same machinery, with different parameters, expresses every related rule:

MechanicSpecialisation
CapsYour team is the trumping team; if you are the Trumper, your information set includes folded-card observations.
External CapsYour team is the non-trumping team; the information set excludes folded-card observations. Same predicate, smaller info set, automatically more lenient.
Absolute HandCaps obligation evaluated at the start of round 1, before any plays. Your information is your hand and the public bidding history.
Claim BalanceReplace “win every remaining round” with “reach the points threshold.” World enumeration is identical.

Absolute Hand is the round-1 case of Caps. Claim Balance is Caps with a different terminal predicate. One framework, four mechanics.

Walking the worlds at the table

You don’t enumerate worlds explicitly in real play — you reason about the structure of the world set: “all remaining tops are mine; opponents are out of the only suit I lack; my partner’s last card is forced.” The formalism is what scrutiny appeals to when an argument is contested. To rehearse the discipline, work the worked example in the Caps section above through these steps:

  1. Inventory. List the cards still unaccounted for — the cards in the pack minus your hand minus the public record (and, if you are the Trumper, minus what you saw face-down).
  2. Constrain. Apply suit-exhaustion. Drop any seat-suit pair that the public record forbids.
  3. Witness. Pick the order you would announce. For each card in that order, ask: “in every remaining world, what could legally beat this play?” If the answer is “nothing”, you have your witness for that round.
  4. Stress-test. If any step depends on a partner’s choice rather than a forced play, you are not yet obligated. Wait for the next observation event and try again.

If you can name the witness order and survive every “but what if the opponent holds X” challenge by pointing to a rule (suit-exhaustion, the closed-trump fold rule, hand size, or a deduction chain through forced follows), you have Caps. If you cannot, you don’t yet.

Two ways to lose a Caps argument under scrutiny

In practice, contested Caps calls fail in one of two ways:

  • A consistent world breaks the witness. The opposition produces a card distribution that respects everything publicly known and in which the announced order loses a round. The call collapses. (This is most common when the caller has tacitly assumed the location of a key card — e.g. the last trump — that the public record does not pin down.)
  • A legal opponent strategy breaks the witness. The world is fixed but the opponent demonstrates a legal play that beats a round — typically a discard or a fold the caller did not consider. (This is more common in External Caps, where the caller has less visibility and may have miscounted exhaustion.)

Both failures point at the same root cause: the witness was a plausible sweep rather than a guaranteed one. The formal test is the difference.

Scoring

Each team begins with 10 stone. The first team to give away all their stone wins the match — giving stone is good, receiving stone is bad.

At the end of each game, both teams count the card points in their won rounds. The betting team must win points equal to or greater than their bid. If they succeed, they give stone to the opponents (moving closer to winning the match). If they fall short, they receive stone from the opponents (moving further from winning).

Note on thresholds: The betting team’s threshold is simply their bid (e.g. a bid of 160 requires 160+ points). The opposing team’s threshold is 304 − bid + 1 (e.g. opposing a 160 bid requires 145+ points). These are mutually exclusive — there is no possibility of a tie, since the total points in the pack is 304.

In-Game Penalties

Certain behaviours attract penalties during a game as they unfairly divulge information or alter the course of play. The standard penalty is 1 stone received by the offending team (i.e. the opposing team gives 1 stone to the offenders, which is bad for the offenders). A penalty applies if the 3 non-offending players reach consensus that one is warranted. If consensus is not reached, the case is adjourned and no penalty applies — penalties are sportsmanship-driven and not strict-liability.

There is a fair bit of pride involved in this game, and good sportsmanship is expected. Beginner players may be given more leeway, but intermediate-to-advanced players should expect stricter enforcement. The lists below are non-exhaustive — the principle is the rule, not the list.

Examples of Penalisable Behaviour (1 stone)

  • Telling your partner how to play (e.g. “Play the Jack of Spades”).
  • Revealing information about your own hand (e.g. “I have the Jack of that suit”).
  • Referencing information from rounds other than the most recent (e.g. on the fourth round, saying “Clubs, Diamonds, then Hearts were played”).
  • Divulging hidden information — cuts, minuses, points in stacks — directly or indirectly (e.g. “opponent cut low”, or “we’ve won 61 points”).
  • Signalling to encourage a particular play (e.g. coughing to signal throwing, pre-agreed code words).
  • Deducing and sharing information from your own hand that others cannot access (e.g. “Player X has no more Clubs — I’m holding all of them”).
  • Playing cuts or minuses unfolded in Closed Trump games.
  • The dealer shuffling the pack in any way after the opposition has cut (or declined to cut).
  • Falsely calling Spoilt Trumps (the opposition did in fact hold trumps).

Severe Penalties (automatic loss + 1 stone)

Some behaviours are so egregious that the offending team automatically loses the game — regardless of the points collected — and receives 1 additional stone on top of the normal loss for their bid:

  • Playing a non-led suit when you hold a card of the led suit (i.e. reneging/revoking).
  • Wrongly claiming balance. Claim balance is an optional house exercise where, having lost one or more rounds, a player asserts that the remaining points are guaranteed sufficient to meet their team’s threshold. It is analogous to Caps, but for the points threshold rather than rounds. If the claim doesn’t hold under scrutiny (the team could still lose another round and fall short), the severe penalty applies.

This list is non-exhaustive.

Glossary

Terms are ordered to minimise forward references. Terms in bold within definitions refer to other glossary entries.

Pack
The 32 playable cards (7–A of all suits).
Stone
The 20 non-playable cards (2–6 of all suits) used as point indicators. Separated by colour, each team holds a pile of 10 stone at the start of a match.
Round
A single trick — all four players play one card each. The winner of the round collects the cards.
Game
A series of 8 rounds, after which stone is exchanged based on the outcome.
Match
A series of games. The first team to give away all 10 stone wins the match.
Revolution
A series of matches where all combinations of players play against each other to determine the overall winner.
Dealer
The player who shuffles and deals. The player to the dealer’s left cuts; the player to the dealer’s right has priority for the first round of betting and play. Between games, the dealer rotates one seat anticlockwise.
Priority
The right to lead a round (i.e. play the first card). The player to the dealer’s right has priority for the first round. Thereafter, the winner of each round gains priority for the next.
Led Suit
The suit of the first card played in a round. All other players must follow this suit if able.
Trump Suit
The suit chosen by the Trumper that takes precedence over all other suits. Often shortened to just “Trump.”
Trump Card
The specific card placed face down by the Trumper to indicate the Trump Suit. Subject to special rules regarding when it can be played.
Trumper
The player with the highest bid. They choose the Trump Card and thus the Trump Suit.
Bid / Bet
A player’s declaration of how many points they believe their team can win. Also refers to the act of declaring such a number.
Pass
Declining to bid on your turn. Counts as a speech.
Partner (action) House Rule — restricted
Asking your partner to bid in your place. Available only in 4-card betting, only by the player to dealer’s right or the player across from dealer, only on their first speech. Counts as a speech for both players. The partnered player’s bid (or pass) is treated as their first speech.
Skip
A silent turn in betting where a player is bypassed without bidding or passing. Two cases: (1) the partnered player’s normal turn is skipped exactly once after they bid via partnering; (2) the current highest bidder is skipped on every subsequent turn until another player overtakes the bid. Skips do not advance or reset the consecutive-pass counter.
First Speech
A player’s first opportunity to bid, pass, or partner in a given betting phase (4-card or 8-card separately). A bid/pass placed via a partner action counts as the partnered player’s first speech.
Threshold
The number of points a team needs to win. For the betting team, this is their bid. For the opposing team, this is 304 − bid + 1.
Open Trump
A mode of play where the Trump suit is known to all players. All cards are played face up. Declared by the Trumper before round 1.
Closed Trump
The default mode where the Trump Card remains face down. Players who cannot follow the led suit play their cards face down.
Fold
To lay a card face down — used for the Trump Card, cuts, and minuses.
Cut
Playing a card of the Trump Suit face down when unable to follow the Led Suit, in an attempt to win the round. In Closed Trump, this involves guessing the Trump Suit. The Trumper’s only face-down trump-suit play is the folded Trump card itself — in-hand trump-suit cards may not be folded by the Trumper.
Overcut
When multiple players attempt to cut and more than one plays a trump-suit card, the highest Trump wins the round and is said to have overcut the others. All trump-suit cuts in the round are revealed; non-trump folded cards remain hidden.
Missing the Cut
Attempting to cut but playing the wrong suit (not the Trump Suit). The card has no power.
Minus
In Closed Trump, when a player (typically the Trumper) cannot follow the Led Suit and chooses to play a non-Trump, non-led-suit card face down. This concedes the round but may be strategically useful (e.g. discarding constraints while the partner is winning).
Caps
Winning all 8 rounds in a game. Must be called at the first opportunity where a player can guarantee the outcome — through their own cards and any outcomes deducible from the rules, but not relying on any player’s choice. Cannot be called after the final card of round 8.
Correct Caps
Calling Caps at the first opportunity, correctly. Rewards +1 stone if called before the start of Round 7; no bonus thereafter.
Late Caps
Calling Caps after the first opportunity, OR winning all 8 rounds without ever calling and missing a first opportunity identifiable in scrutiny. Punished by loss + 1 stone (game flipped to loss).
Wrong/Early Caps
Calling Caps incorrectly, too early, or deliberately throwing a round to conceal a Caps situation. Punished by 5 stone.
External Caps
Caps called by the non-Trumping team. Same correctness test as regular Caps but with a more lenient “first opportunity” standard given limited information. Does not apply to PCC. Outcomes mirror regular Caps in structure but framed against the betting team’s loss.
Absolute Hand House Rule
A hand so strong that the holder is guaranteed to win all 8 rounds before play begins, regardless of priority order. Holder may declare pre-round-1 (goodwill) or opposition may claim post-game during scrutiny. Triggers a pass-on with no stone exchanged. Exceptionally rare (<1% of hands).
Exhausted Trumps
A situation where the Trumper holds all remaining cards of the Trump Suit. If the Trumper has priority and leads Trump, they must lead all remaining Trump from their hand before playing any other suit. Applies in both Open and Closed Trump (does not apply in PCC).
Spoilt Trumps
When the opposition holds zero Trump cards between them from the deal. Triggers a pass-on with no stone exchanged, provided it is declared before the last card is played. False call: 1-stone penalty.
Honest House Rule term
A bid of 220 on 8 cards. Increments above are Honest 5 (225), Honest 10 (230), … up to Honest 25 (245), then 250, then PCC.
PCC (Partner Closed Caps)
The highest possible bid. The Trumper plays alone (partner sits out), must win all 8 rounds, and plays Open Trump (with the freedom to lead any card on round 1 — the only open-trump bid not bound to lead trump). Caps and External Caps do not apply.
Redeal
The same dealer reshuffles and deals again. Triggered by a 4-card hand below 15 points (priority player or their partnered partner).
Pass-on
The deal moves to the next dealer (anticlockwise). Triggered by 4-card betting ending with no bid, an 8-card hand below 25 points (declared on first 8-card speech), Spoilt Trumps, or Absolute Hand.
Hand
The cards a player holds at any point during play. At the start of play, this is 8 cards (or 7, for the Trumper, with the 8th being the folded Trump card on the table). Hands shrink by one card per round.
Points
The value of cards as given by the face value / point value table. Determines hand strength, thresholds, and triggers for Redeal/Pass-on.
Claim Balance House Rule
An optional house exercise: having lost one or more rounds, a player asserts that the remaining points are guaranteed to meet their team’s threshold. Analogous to Caps but for the points threshold rather than rounds. A wrong claim incurs the severe penalty (auto-loss + 1 stone).
Scrutiny
The post-play deliberation phase. Both teams count points, verify Caps/External Caps timing, resolve penalty disputes, and exchange stone. Caps cannot be newly called during scrutiny — only verified or retroactively flagged as Late.
